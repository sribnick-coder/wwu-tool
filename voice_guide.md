# A-Street Weekly Wrap-Up: Voice & Format Guide

This document describes the editorial voice, format conventions, and content standards for the A-Street Weekly Wrap-Up, derived from analysis of multiple issues. It is intended as a reference for AI-assisted drafting.

---

## Purpose and Audience

The Weekly Wrap-Up is a curated digest of PreK–12 education news, research, and opinion sent to the A-Street community (investors, operators, advisors, portfolio founders, and education sector leaders). It is not a neutral news aggregation service — it is a lightly opinionated, investor-informed scan of the field, written by people who care deeply about outcomes and have a point of view on what matters.

---

## Voice and Tone

**Informed but accessible.** Entries assume a reader who is knowledgeable about K–12 education policy, edtech, and research — but not a specialist in every sub-topic. Jargon is used when it's industry-standard (HQIM, NAEP, Science of Reading, E-Rate), but not gratuitously.

**Analytical, not just descriptive.** The best entries don't just say what an article covers — they explain *why it matters* or *what the tension is*. A summary of a research paper surfaces the key finding and its implication. A policy piece captures the argument and its stakes.

**Opinionated but not polemical.** A-Street has a perspective — evidence-based instruction, outcomes-focused investment, skepticism of hype without proof — but entries don't editorialize heavily. The framing does the work. Phrases like "the article highlights the need for evidence" or "intensifying scrutiny" signal a view without stating it.

**Concise and complete.** Entries are typically 2–4 sentences. Every word should earn its place. The goal is to give the reader enough to decide whether to click through — not to replace reading the original.

**No hedging, no throat-clearing.** Entries begin directly with the substance. No "This article discusses…" or "In this piece…" openings.

---

## Structural Format

Each entry follows this pattern:

```
**Article Title:** [2–4 sentence summary with A-Street framing.] ([Source Name with hyperlink])
```

- The **title is bolded** and followed by a colon and a space.
- The title is usually the article's actual headline, sometimes shortened.
- The source name (publication or author platform) appears in parentheses at the end as a hyperlink.
- Entries are separated by blank lines (no numbered list, no bullets).
- No subheadings within the digest body.

**Multiple related items** are occasionally grouped under a single logical entry — e.g., two data releases about the same topic treated as a cluster with a single source block. This is done sparingly and only when the items genuinely illuminate each other.

---

## Summary Writing Rules

### Do
- Lead with the *key finding, argument, or news hook* — not with the author's name or publication.
- Name authors and organizations when they are notable, credentialed, or relevant to A-Street's network (e.g., "Robin Lake from CRPE," "Mike Petrilli of Fordham").
- Call out A-Street connections explicitly: portfolio companies, advisors, and EIRs are flagged by name and relationship (e.g., "Amplify's Dan Meyer, an A-Street portfolio company"; "A-Street Executive-in-Residence Jordan Meranus").
- Include a direct quote (in quotation marks) when the original language is especially sharp, memorable, or impossible to paraphrase without losing meaning. Use sparingly — one per entry at most.
- Note when findings contradict conventional wisdom or popular narratives (e.g., "while the pandemic is often blamed, the data shows the slide began in 2015").
- Frame research entries around practical implication, not just methodology.

### Don't
- Don't reproduce the article's framing uncritically if it's oversimplified or contested. Light skepticism is appropriate.
- Don't write "The article argues…" or "The author believes…" as filler — use a tighter construction.
- Don't editorialize with value-laden adjectives ("troubling," "exciting," "shocking") — let the data or framing do the work.
- Don't pad to a minimum length. A clean two-sentence entry is better than a bloated four-sentence one.
- Don't use bullet points or sub-lists within an entry.

## Thematic Coverage Areas

The digest consistently covers these domains — all filtered through an investment and outcomes lens:

- **AI in education** — evidence base, adoption pace, product quality, trust/transparency
- **Edtech policy** — phone/screen bans, E-Rate, federal and state regulation
- **Curriculum and instruction** — HQIM, Science of Reading, assessment, literacy
- **Edtech market dynamics** — funding landscape, vendor consolidation, sales practices, cybersecurity
- **Student outcomes and equity** — achievement data (NAEP, SEDA), demographic trends, enrollment shifts
- **School finance** — per-pupil spending, budget dynamics, tax credit scholarships
- **Higher education** — only when directly relevant to K–12 pipeline or investment themes

## A-Street Lens: What Gets Included and Why

Not every article that is interesting gets included. The digest filters for pieces that are:

1. **Relevant to portfolio companies** — research or policy that affects Amplify, Innovamat, or other portfolio companies gets priority
2. **Relevant to investment thesis** — edtech evidence, market dynamics, AI adoption, curriculum quality
3. **Challenging conventional wisdom** — findings that complicate the simple narrative are more interesting than those that confirm it
4. **Actionable for educators or operators** — practical guidance for teachers, district leaders, or product builders
5. **Representative of diverse perspectives** — the digest explicitly aims to include a range of views, not just those A-Street agrees with

## Tone Calibration Examples

**Too neutral (avoid):**
> "A new study from RAND examines reading instruction in secondary ELA classrooms and finds differences in book assignment patterns."

**Too opinionated (avoid):**
> "RAND's damning new research confirms that disadvantaged students are being shortchanged on literature — a failure our sector needs to urgently address."

**Right register:**
> "RAND findings from their Spring 2025 American Instructional Resources Survey show that teachers serving more historically disadvantaged students assigned fewer full books. The study advocates for more full books in ELA curricula to enhance literacy instruction and student outcomes."

---

## What AI Should NOT Do

When drafting entries for this digest, AI should not:

- Invent A-Street connections (portfolio flagging, advisor relationships) that have not been confirmed
- Summarize articles it has not read — for paywalled content, flag that the entry is based on headline/preview only
- Use generic "thought leadership" language ("game-changing," "paradigm shift," "revolutionize")
- Reproduce copyrighted text verbatim
- Include articles that are purely political without an education policy angle
- Add commentary beyond what the framing requires — the digest is not a blog post

---

## Final Assembly

The digest body is preceded by:
- The header "Headlines" (H1)
- Two blank lines

Each entry is separated by a blank line. No numbered list. No section breaks.

The digest closes with the standard boilerplate:

> *Thank you for reading the A-Street Weekly Wrap-Up, a collection of notable news, announcements, and opinions gathered from a broad scan of PreK-12 media. We curate the selection based on what we think might be most relevant, thought-provoking, and helpful to the A-Street community. We endeavor to include a variety of perspectives and views beyond just our own. Questions, comments, or suggestions? We'd love to hear from you at hello@astreet.com.*
>
> *Was this forwarded to you? Please subscribe!*
