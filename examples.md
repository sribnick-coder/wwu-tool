# A-Street Weekly Wrap-Up: Example Entries

This file contains real entries from past A-Street Weekly Wrap-Up issues. It is used as few-shot context in Claude API prompts for summary generation. The goal is for new AI-generated summaries to match the voice, structure, and editorial sensibility illustrated here.

When generating a new summary, study these examples for:
- How entries open (variety of leads — never the same construction twice in a row)
- How data and findings are framed (specific, not vague)
- How opinion pieces are handled (author's argument surfaced, but not always the only frame)
- How both perspectives on an issue are represented where relevant
- How portfolio connections and A-Street relationships are flagged
- How the source link appears at the end: **(Publication Name)** with hyperlink to article URL

---

## Section 1: Research and data entries

These examples show how to lead with the key finding and implication, not methodology.

---

**Why U.S. Test Scores Are in a 'Generation-Long Decline':** The Stanford Education Data Archive (SEDA) released its latest student achievement data. The results are sobering: nationally, eighty-three percent of school districts have lower reading scores than a decade ago, and math scores are down in seventy percent of districts. While the pandemic is often blamed, the data shows that the slide began in 2015. (SEDA)

> *Note: This entry leads with the data point, not the source or author. The key counter-narrative — "the slide began before the pandemic" — is surfaced explicitly because it challenges the dominant explanation.*

---

**The Education Scorecard:** The product of a collaboration between Harvard, Stanford, and Dartmouth, the Scorecard links state test results to track district-level changes and reveals a "U-shaped recovery" — high and low-income districts have had the largest gains, with middle-income districts lagging behind. States that have implemented comprehensive early literacy reforms (TN, MS, LA) are seeing improvements, affirming reporting of a "Southern Surge." (Education Opportunity Project)

> *Note: Two related data releases (SEDA + the Scorecard) were grouped as a cluster in the original issue, each as its own short entry. This is appropriate when items illuminate the same story from different angles.*

---

**Reading Full Books in Secondary English Language Arts Classrooms:** RAND findings from their Spring 2025 American Instructional Resources Survey show that teachers serving more historically disadvantaged students assigned fewer full books. Teachers who assigned full books more often did so beyond what their curriculum required. The study advocates for more full books in ELA curricula to enhance literacy instruction and student outcomes. (RAND)

---

**Do School Cellphone Bans Work? New Study Finds Mixed Results:** A new national study of school cellphone bans finds that while locking phones away engenders rare bipartisan support and dramatically reduces student phone use in class, the academic and behavioral gains are far smaller than many advocates expected. Drawing on data from roughly 4,600 schools using Yondr lockable pouches, researchers found little impact on test scores, attendance, or bullying, though student well-being and discipline showed modest improvement over time. (NY Times)

> *Note: The headline poses a question; the entry answers it directly and specifically — don't leave the reader in suspense. The nuance (bipartisan support, reduced phone use, but limited academic impact) is preserved.*

---

**A Major Paper Claiming AI is Good for Students Just Got Retracted:** A paper published in Nature touting the benefits of ChatGPT has been retracted, intensifying scrutiny around the evidence base for AI in education at a moment when schools are rapidly adopting new tools. (Futurism)

> *Note: Short entry — two sentences is sufficient when the news is self-contained and the implication is clear. Don't pad.*

---

**The Evidence Base on AI in K-12:** Stanford's SCALE initiative released its 2026 review of AI educational tools. The report emphasizes that high-quality K-12 studies remain very limited and that the scant research available is focused on "constrained conditions (such as a one-time 20-minute experiment) and focuses on short-term outcomes." (Stanford SCALE Initiative)

---

**'A' Grades Are Suddenly Everywhere Since the Arrival of ChatGPT:** New research suggests that professors in "AI-exposed classes" (those with writing assignments as homework) gave out thirty percent more As. Employers seeking to use GPAs as a hiring metric may find the measure less reliable. This week, Harvard faculty voted on three proposals in an effort to curb grade inflation at the school. (WSJ)

> *Note: Three related facts woven into one tight entry. The WSJ is paywalled — see paywall handling guidance in requirements.*

---

**TX Public Schools See First Non-Pandemic Enrollment Drop in Decades:** While the precise cause remains unknown, 76,000 fewer children are enrolled in Texas public schools this year and the state's enrollment is expected to "drop by roughly 500,000 in the next four to five years." Anti-immigration rhetoric is one possible factor, as eighty-one percent of the enrollment drop is Hispanic students, who make up fifty-three percent of the state public school population. (The Texas Tribune)

> *Note: State-level enrollment story treated as nationally significant because TX is a bellwether state. The demographic breakdown is specific and relevant — include it.*

## Section 2: Policy and opinion entries

These entries show how to handle opinion pieces without reducing them to "Author X argues." The framing surfaces what the piece enters — the broader debate — and often includes a counter-perspective or tension.

---

**Federal Tax-Credit Scholarship Update:** New York Democratic Governor Hochul signals that she will opt into the Education Freedom Tax Credit. Mike Petrilli of the Fordham Institute argues that "blue states should join their red state peers," and the Washington Post Editorial Board opines similarly. To date, twenty-nine governors have indicated they will participate in the program. The American Experiment, a Minnesota-based public policy organization, published a list of common "myths" about the tax credit in hopes that holdout governors — including Minnesota's own — will join. (SCHOOLED)

> *Note: Multiple voices and positions woven into one entry without the entry itself taking a side. The summary reads as a status update, not an op-ed.*

---

**Against Curriculum Dilution (and What To Do Instead):** Instructional coach Michele Caracappa addresses the implementation barrier that arises when teachers modify their provided curricula — often because they believe it doesn't meet students' needs — and advocates for what she terms "curriculum fortification." She calls on school and system leaders to foster high expectations and provide professional development that supports "maintaining the level of challenge within the curriculum as written." (Reading to Lead)

> *Note: This entry does open with the author's name, but it's used to establish credibility ("instructional coach"), not just to attribute an argument. The entry surfaces the legitimate tension (teachers have real reasons for modifying curricula) before landing on the author's position.*

---

**States: Don't Ban EdTech, Do This Instead:** Jessica Baghian from Watershed Advisors calls on state education agencies to "step up and lead on edtech" by identifying specific problems, curating the marketplace for appropriate solutions, and holding vendors and districts accountable for outcomes. (LinkedIn)

---

**Opinion | More Colleges Are Closing. It's About Time:** According to projections, more than 400 private, nonprofit colleges are at risk of closing or merging within a decade. Economist Roland Fryer argues that while the closures are not painless, this is good news — "the schools doing the most for upward mobility aren't the ones closing." He calls for market acceleration so students can identify schools that will best provide a strong return on their investment. (WSJ)

> *Note: The provocative headline is preserved. The entry gives Fryer's argument in his own framing, including a direct quote, without the digest itself endorsing the position.*

---

**Negative Polarization is Hamstringing Education Reform:** Andy Rotherham emphasizes the need for a "bipartisan or robustly cross-partisan environment" in education reform, passionately calling for the alliance of more "strange bedfellows" who can "engage in the messy work of getting something done for the people you purport to care about and who deserve far better." (Eduwonk)

---

**Opinion | Some States Are Banning Much More Than Phones in Schools. That's a Huge Mistake:** Richard Culatta, CEO of ISTE+ASCD, warns that efforts to curb student phone use are expanding into broader technology bans that may unintentionally restrict access to valuable digital learning tools. He advocates for more targeted approaches, arguing that sweeping policies risk stifling innovation and widening opportunity gaps. (The 74)

> *Note: Opinion piece attributed clearly, but the entry opens with the *consequence* Culatta is worried about (expanding bans, not just phones) rather than "Culatta argues." The reader understands the stakes before the attribution.*

---

**Keep AI Out of American Classrooms:** Jonah Goldberg, a Senior Fellow at AEI, argues that students need to learn by doing and worries that "AI removes the doing." He responds to the argument that AI can promote "upskilling" by observing that workers become highly skilled after first "doing the work" themselves. (AEI)

> *Note: This entry opens with the author name and affiliation — appropriate here because the piece is a clear opinion from a named, credentialed commentator and the framing is inherently about the author's position. Used sparingly.*

---

**The Problem Isn't Screens. It's That We've Turned Learning Into a Game No One Wants to Play:** Games for Change argues that the problem with classroom technology is not screens or games themselves, but shallow "learning games" that are just quizzes with points, timers, and leaderboards. The authors advocate for better-designed learning experiences that create meaningful challenges and build skills — such as decision-making and iteration — which standardized tests often miss. (Games for Change)

## Section 3: A-Street portfolio and relationship entries

These entries show exactly how to flag portfolio companies and A-Street relationships.

---

**Built to Last | Preparing for Success After an Acquisition:** A-Street Executive-in-Residence Jordan Meranus reflects on why promising education companies experience mission drift post acquisition, and offers guidance to founders, investors, and acquirers on how to preserve long-term value and a focus on outcomes. Drawing on conversations with entrepreneurs and lessons from his experience as the co-founder of Ellevation, Jordan explores what it takes to build companies designed not just for exit, but for enduring impact. (LinkedIn | A-Street)

> *Note: EIR relationship flagged explicitly in the first sentence. The entry reads as a genuine endorsement — appropriate when it is A-Street content. The dual source link (LinkedIn | A-Street) points to both platforms.*

---

**Everyone in Edtech Should Show Their Work:** Amplify's Dan Meyer, an A-Street portfolio company, implores edtech executives to post a video of a screen-enabled lesson to demonstrate the merits of tech-enabled instruction while enhancing transparency around AI-driven products. Meyer contends that clearer explanations of how tools work — and where they fall short — could help rebuild educator trust and improve product quality over time. (Mathworlds)

> *Note: Portfolio connection flagged in the first breath: "Amplify's Dan Meyer, an A-Street portfolio company." The entry goes on to engage with the substance of the argument, not just the affiliation.*

## Section 4: Industry and market entries

---

**Instructure Is Risking the Trust That Built Canvas:** The Canvas cyberattack impacting an estimated 9,000 institutions and up to 275 million user records continues to raise questions about how much sensitive student information major edtech vendors now hold — and how prepared schools are when those systems are breached. Phil Hill, an edtech market analyst, discusses how this incident erodes public trust for the sector, arguing that "Instructure treated a vendor-level security crisis primarily as a status-page incident." (On EdTech)

---

**The Ed Tech Email Flood:** Chalkbeat asked five superintendents to share sales pitches emailed to them in a single day in March. In total, they shared ninety messages from seventy-nine companies. The superintendents reported that crowded inboxes and a glut of choices — in an industry with little regulation — "isn't helping school district leaders make smart choices." The article highlights the need for evidence both to help vendors distinguish their products in a crowded marketplace and to inform district buyers. (Chalkbeat)

---

**The Revolt Against i-Ready:** The product, which reaches an estimated 14 million students each year, faces "mounting backlash from parents, students and teachers who see it as a waste of time." Critics cite a lack of evidence of impact; teachers bemoan how much instructional time the program requires; and students complain the animations are repetitive. Curriculum Associates released a series of blog posts highlighting the benefits of the software. (NBC News)

> *Note: Three stakeholder perspectives (parents/students, teachers, company) woven into one entry. The company's response is included — this is the "both perspectives" principle applied to a market/product story.*

---

**Some Parents Don't Want Their Kids to Use Tech at School. But Districts are Pushing Back:** As edtech backlash grows, some school boards are affirming that opting out of technology is not always an option. This story, focused on a Philadelphia suburb, acknowledges community concerns while highlighting the tension districts face as technology is woven into so many aspects of students' education. (AP)

---

**How YouTube Took Over the American Classroom:** As schools introduced YouTube for educational content, they have inadvertently "created a gateway for students to get sucked into an infinite scroll of videos on school-issued devices." Futuresource Consulting estimates that YouTube is a top-viewed website on school devices, sometimes accounting for half of student traffic. School districts, parents, and teachers grapple with finding the right balance between the platform's benefits and risks. (WSJ)

---

**Federal "First Look" Financials:** A new NCES report shows that, for the first time in history, K-12 public education spending passed $1 trillion. Edunomics Lab analyzed available state data, noting that it remains largely variable by state — and that without NCES, it is unclear how or when such data will be collected going forward. (Edunomics Lab)

## Section 5: Short and uplifting entries

Not every entry needs to be weighty. These shorter entries show how to include lighter or smaller-scale stories without padding.

---

**New Law Doubles Elementary Recess Time in OK Schools:** A new law set to go into effect in the fall will require 40 minutes of recess per day for Oklahoma students in grades K–5. The law also prohibits schools from revoking recess as a form of discipline. (Oklahoma Voice)

> *Note: Two sentences, fully self-contained. The second sentence (revoking recess as discipline) adds meaningful context without padding.*

---

**Most Homeschoolers Also Use an Array of Resources, Data Shows:** New data shows that most homeschooling families rely on a mix of curricula, digital tools, and supplemental programs. This hybrid approach points to a more modular education landscape and growing demand for flexible learning options. (The 74)

## Section 6: Science of Reading and curriculum entries

These entries appear frequently and show how to handle implementation vs. research stories distinctly.

---

**A Science of Reading Progress Report:** The Fordham Institute released a new report indicating that the national dialogue has evolved from debating the adoption of the Science of Reading to analyzing how its implementation is actually progressing. Key insights reveal that educators in high-poverty environments often feel conflicted regarding phonics, training provided by teacher preparation programs may be "actively harmful" to teachers' grasp of evidence-based methods, and many teachers continue to use curricula unsupported by research. (Fordham Institute)

---

**Chad Aldeman** emphasized that the major takeaway of the report concerns "what's left to do." He notes that the highest-need students are most at risk of not receiving high-quality, research-backed reading instruction. (Aldeman on Education)

> *Note: This entry followed the Fordham entry directly in the original issue — a second take on the same report from a different commentator. This is appropriate when a response piece adds meaningfully to the picture. In these cases, lead with the author's name since the context was set in the previous entry.*

---

**How One Arizona District Used Elementary Learning to Shape High School Results:** Christie Olsen, director of student achievement in Lake Havasu Unified School District, shares her district's experience implementing HQIM. The process, which she admits was "excruciatingly challenging," is beginning to show results at the high school level. (The 74)

> *Note: Practitioner voice ("she admits it was excruciatingly challenging") makes the entry more credible than a purely positive summary. Include the candor.*

---

**We Taught Students to Read. We Still Can't Tell if They Understand:** ANET shines a light on the "comprehension chasm" — assessments cannot show teachers what they need to help students become skilled readers. ANET stresses the urgent need for high-quality instructional assessment (HQIA) that can provide teachers with "diagnostic architecture that tells a teacher what is actually breaking down." (ANET)

---

**Time for Schools to Stop Testing Kids Like It's the 1990s. AI Can Show the Way:** Former Massachusetts Governor Jane Swift advocates for assessments to move from being an "autopsy" at the end of a given school year to a "compass," utilizing new technologies and offering more frequent, interim assessments that can inform instruction. (The 74)

## Prompt guidance for AI summary generation

When generating a new entry, ask:

1. **What is the single most interesting or important thing about this article?** Lead with that — not with the author's name or publication.
2. **Does this article enter a larger debate?** If so, name the debate and at least acknowledge the other side.
3. **Is there a number, a quote, or a specific detail that makes the story concrete?** Use it. Vague summaries ("the article explores the challenges of...") are not useful.
4. **Is this good news, bad news, or complicated?** The tone should reflect that — don't flatten nuance into neutrality.
5. **Is an A-Street portfolio company or advisor mentioned?** Flag it explicitly in the first sentence.
6. **Would our audience — investors, operators, education leaders — find this actionable, provocative, or illuminating?** If not, reconsider whether to include it.
7. **Is the entry 2–4 sentences?** If it's longer, cut. If it's one sentence, add the implication or the context.
8. **Does it end with the source in the correct format?** `(Publication Name)` as a hyperlink — nothing else.
